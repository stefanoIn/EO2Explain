\subsection{Full specification of your proposal}
\label{subsec:specification}

The project consists of the use of a symbolic multi-agent system for monitoring environmental changes using satellite-derived Earth Observation (EO) indicators. The project integrates techniques from Symbolic and Distributed Artificial Intelligence (S\&DAI) and Natural Language Processing (NLP), with the goal of demonstrating how symbolic reasoning and language technologies can be combined to interpret EO data in a transparent and structured way.

The workflow of the project is divided into three conceptual levels.

The first level is the EO level, where a small dataset of satellite-derived EO indicators is defined. In the current implementation, these indicators are extracted directly from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery for a limited number of wildfire and flood events. The extracted indicators include values such as vegetation loss percentage, burned area percentage, and water increase percentage.

The second level is a symbolic multi-agent architecture implemented in Jason~\cite{bordini2007jason}. The system includes three agent types: a ForestAgent responsible for reasoning about vegetation-related indicators, a WaterAgent responsible for reasoning about water-related indicators, and a CoordinatorAgent responsible for collecting the conclusions produced by the other agents and coordinating the final output.

The third level is the NLP layer, implemented in Python. This layer starts from a structured semantic explanation payload exported by the Jason MAS, transforms the raw Jason JSON into a cleaner semantic JSON representation, populates an OWL ontology, and finally generates readable event reports.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Earth Observation data provide a powerful tool for monitoring environmental changes such as floods and wildfires. In recent years, sub-symbolic Artificial Intelligence methods, especially machine learning and deep learning approaches, have become widely used in the EO field because of their strong predictive and pattern-recognition capabilities. These methods are particularly effective for processing large volumes of satellite imagery and detecting complex spatial patterns that may be difficult to capture with manual analysis alone.

However, in delicate application areas such as hazard detection, computational performance is not the only requirement. Assessments of floods, wildfires, and other environmental risks often involve uncertainty, incomplete observations, temporal limitations, and case-specific caveats. In such contexts, explainability and transparency become as important as predictive capability, because the decision-making process must remain understandable, traceable, and open to inspection. A system that produces a strong classification but cannot clearly justify how that conclusion was reached may be less suitable in settings where reliability, accountability, and interpretation matter

This project  explores the integration of satellite-derived environmental indicators with symbolic multi-agent reasoning and Natural Language Processing techniques. Instead of treating EO indicators only as numerical outputs, the system interprets them through explicit logical reasoning performed by specialized agents and communicates the conclusions through natural language explanations. In this way, the project does not reject the importance of sub-symbolic AI in EO, but focuses on a complementary perspective in which the reasoning path, the handling of caveats, and the interpretability of the final assessment are central design goals.

The overall objective is to build a prototype system capable of transforming Earth Observation evidence into structured interpretations that remain transparent, explainable, and semantically traceable.

\section{Architecture Overview}

The EO2Explain system is organized into three conceptual layers, each responsible for a distinct stage of the processing pipeline.

The first layer is the \textbf{EO layer}, which extracts environmental indicators from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery. This layer transforms raw spectral data into quantitative measures of environmental change, such as vegetation loss and water increase, providing the numerical evidence for subsequent reasoning.

The second layer consists of a \textbf{symbolic multi-agent system} implemented in Jason. Here, EO indicators are represented as symbolic beliefs and processed through logical rules to infer environmental events. 

The third layer is the \textbf{Natural Language Processing (NLP) layer}. This module receives the semantic explanation payload produced by the MAS, transforms it into a cleaner JSON representation, populates the ontology, and generates report-ready textual outputs. Rather than performing hazard reasoning itself, the Python layer acts as a post-reasoning semantic and reporting component.

A lightweight Flask-based UI is built on top of this pipeline, allowing users to upload Sentinel-2 bands, provide optional contextual assumptions, and inspect both the resulting semantic report and the MAS trace.

The EO layer is based on a small dataset of representative environmental events derived from Sentinel-2 imagery. In the current implementation, six events were selected: three wildfire events and three flood events. For each event, one image acquired before the event and one image acquired after the event were used in order to compute change-based indicators.

\subsection{Sentinel-2 Data and Bands}

The analysis uses Sentinel-2 Level-2A imagery. The used spectral bands are the B03 (Green), B04 (Red), B08 (Near Infrared) and the B12 (Short-Wave Infrared).
These bands allow the computation of spectral indices commonly used for vegetation monitoring, burn detection, and water detection.

\subsection{Processing Workflow}

The EO preprocessing is implemented through Python scripts that extract the indicators described in Section~5.5. Across the flood and wildfire workflows, the processing pipeline consists of loading the required GeoTIFF bands, aligning them when necessary, computing spectral indices, deriving binary masks from threshold-based conditions, computing event-level summary statistics, and exporting the results to CSV tables.

In the flood workflow, the script computes NDWI-based indicators and derives water and newly flooded area masks. In the wildfire workflow, the script computes NDVI, NBR, and dNBR, resamples the SWIR band to ensure alignment with the other inputs, and derives vegetation-loss and burned-area indicators.

\subsection{Wildfire Indicators}

Wildfire analysis was based on the computation of NDVI, NBR, and dNBR.

\subsubsection{Normalized Difference Vegetation Index}

\begin{equation}
NDVI = \frac{B08 - B04}{B08 + B04}
\end{equation}

NDVI, originally introduced by Rouse et al.~\cite{rouse1974}, measures the condition of the vegetation. Lower NDVI values after an event indicate vegetation damage.

\subsubsection{Normalized Burn Ratio}

\begin{equation}
NBR = \frac{B08 - B12}{B08 + B12}
\end{equation}

Burn severity is estimated using NBR and its temporal difference dNBR, following the remote-sensing burn severity framework described by Key and Benson~\cite{key2006}:

\begin{equation}
dNBR = NBR_{before} - NBR_{after}
\end{equation}

Pixels above a selected threshold are classified as burned.

\subsection{Flood Indicators}

The flood analysis was based on the normalized difference in water index proposed by McFeeters~\cite{mcfeeters1996}.

\begin{equation}
NDWI = \frac{B03 - B08}{B03 + B08}
\end{equation}

NDWI allows the detection of water surfaces and the creation of water masks. Newly flooded areas are detected by comparing water masks before and after the event.

\section{Reasoning Layer}

The EO indicators computed in the previous layer are automatically translated into \texttt{.asl} beliefs by the \texttt{generate\_indicator\_beliefs.py} script. The reasoning layer is implemented in Jason, where numerical EO outputs are no longer treated as isolated measurements. Instead, symbolic EO indicator beliefs are combined with contextual metadata and interpreted through explicit reasoning rules.

This constitutes the core of the system’s explainability: every classification is grounded in a transparent belief structure, a well-defined reasoning path, and a traceable final explanation.

The reasoning layer is structured around three types of agents: a central CoordinatorAgent, a set of hazard-specialized agents (in this project, WaterAgents and ForestAgents), and an ExplanationAgent. 
This limited configuration reflects a design choice rather than a conceptual limitation. Once the atomic structure of the system is defined, the multi-agent system can be readily scaled. In particular, hazard-specialized agents share a common reasoning pattern, which makes them naturally suited for horizontal scaling by simply instantiating additional agents for different hazard types or regions.

A natural extension of this architecture would involve multiple groups of hazard agents, each coordinated by a dedicated CoordinatorAgent. These coordinators could, in turn, be managed by a higher-level coordinator responsible for dispatching tasks across groups. In such a hierarchical configuration, ExplanationAgents—ideally one per group—would generate the final symbolic reports and return them to their respective coordinators for further integration. A more detailed discussion of the scalability properties and limitations of this architecture is provided in the conclusion section.

Although the system was not developed by strictly following a specific agent-oriented methodology, its design is closer to an organisation-oriented approach, such as Gaia~\cite{wooldridge2000gaia}, as it emphasizes role-based decomposition and clearly defined interaction patterns between agents. The CoordinatorAgent, hazard-specialized agents, and ExplanationAgent correspond to distinct organizational roles, and their communication follows structured protocols.

Figure~\ref{fig:sequence_diagram} illustrates the temporal communication flow within the symbolic multi-agent system, using an example interaction between the CoordinatorAgent and the WaterAgent. The CoordinatorAgent serves as the central orchestration component: it dispatches event beliefs to the appropriate hazard-specialized agent, receives the corresponding assessment, and, if necessary, requests additional clarification when the initial evaluation remains uncertain. The finalized symbolic output is then forwarded to the ExplanationAgent for subsequent processing.

\subsection{Belief Base}

The reasoning layer is grounded in a structured belief base that encodes both EO-derived evidence and contextual knowledge. The belief base is organised into four main groups of facts:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Event metadata}, describing the identity, location, temporal context, and hazard type of each event, as well as the mapping to the responsible hazard-specialised agent.
\item \textbf{EO indicator beliefs}, containing the satellite-derived measurements computed in the previous layer, such as NDWI-based water indicators for floods and NDVI/dNBR-based vegetation and burn indicators for wildfires.
\item \textbf{Context beliefs}, capturing additional information relevant to interpretation, including population exposure metrics and observation-quality metadata (e.g., acquisition timing and potential underestimation flags).
\item \textbf{Optional user-input beliefs}, used to store a user-provided severity expectation for post-hoc comparison with the system’s assessment. These beliefs are introduced through the UI layer and do not influence the internal reasoning process.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Event Metadata Beliefs}

Each event is represented through a set of symbolic facts that specify its geographic context, temporal coverage, hazard type, and reliability of the observation timeline. These beliefs are not purely descriptive; they also support later stages of reasoning, particularly in the qualification of uncertainty. For instance, the beliefs \texttt{timeline\_confidence/2}, \texttt{late\_observation\_flag/2}, and \texttt{possible\_underestimation/2} are used to determine whether the available EO evidence may fail to capture the peak intensity of the hazard. This information is subsequently exploited by hazard-specialised agents to introduce caveats or reduce confidence in the final assessment.
In this sense, event metadata act as more than simple labels attached to a case: they directly influence how the reasoning process interprets EO indicators and how conclusions are qualified.

\begin{lstlisting}
event(ahr_valley).
name(ahr_valley, "Ahr Valley").
country(ahr_valley, germany).
region(ahr_valley, rhineland_palatinate).
event_type(ahr_valley, flood).
agent_responsible(ahr_valley, water_agent).
before_date(ahr_valley, "2021-03-30").
after_date(ahr_valley, "2021-07-21").
timeline_confidence(ahr_valley, confirmed).
late_observation_flag(ahr_valley, true).
possible_underestimation(ahr_valley, true).
\end{lstlisting}

The same representation is adopted for wildfire events, with the hazard type and corresponding hazard-specialised agent varying accordingly.

\subsubsection{EO Indicator Beliefs}

The EO indicators extracted from Sentinel-2 imagery are encoded as logical beliefs, making numerical evidence directly accessible to the Jason reasoning rules. This transformation allows the system to operate on quantitative measurements through symbolic predicates, enabling explicit and traceable reasoning.

Flood events are represented through NDWI-based change indicators, capturing both the magnitude of spectral variation and the spatial extent of water expansion:

\begin{lstlisting}
mean_ndwi_before(emilia, -0.5065).
mean_ndwi_after(emilia, -0.3007).
ndwi_change(emilia, 0.2057).
water_increase_pct(emilia, 22.73).
newly_flooded_area_pct(emilia, 22.90).
\end{lstlisting}

These beliefs allow the WaterAgent to reason about the presence and severity of flooding by combining spectral change with areal expansion signals.

Wildfire events are represented through vegetation-loss and burn-related indicators derived from NDVI and NBR:

\begin{lstlisting}
mean_ndvi_before(evros, 0.4516).
mean_ndvi_after(evros, 0.3257).
ndvi_drop(evros, 0.1259).
mean_dnbr(evros, 0.0453).
vegetation_loss_pct(evros, 21.06).
burned_area_pct(evros, 13.85).
\end{lstlisting}

These beliefs enable the ForestAgent to infer fire severity by jointly considering vegetation degradation and burn intensity patterns.

In both cases, EO indicator beliefs serve as the primary evidence layer, bridging the gap between low-level satellite measurements and high-level symbolic interpretation.

\subsubsection{Context Beliefs}

The belief base also includes contextual information that is used during the fusion stage of the reasoning process. In particular, each event is associated with a population exposure class derived from WorldPop data~\cite{worldpop2025}.

\begin{lstlisting}
population_exposure_class(sindh, high).
population_exposure_class(emilia, medium).
population_exposure_class(evros, low).
\end{lstlisting}

These contextual beliefs do not represent hazard evidence on their own. Instead, they are used by the CoordinatorAgent to combine hazard assessments with exposure information in order to derive higher-level interpretations, such as concern level and overall case profile.

This additional symbolic context is essential because the system does not stop at detecting the presence of a hazard. It also evaluates how impactful the situation may be by integrating hazard severity with population exposure, and it adjusts the final interpretation accordingly in terms of concern and confidence.

\subsubsection{Optional User-Input Beliefs}

The architecture also supports an optional user-provided assessment, entered through the UI layer. This information does not influence the internal hazard reasoning process.

Instead, it is represented as a separate symbolic belief that is incorporated only after the reasoning stage, allowing the CoordinatorAgent to compare the user expectation with the inferred result.

\begin{lstlisting}
user_assessment(ahr_valley, severe).
\end{lstlisting}

This design preserves a clear separation between evidence-driven inference and external input. Hazard-specialised agents derive their conclusions exclusively from EO indicators and contextual beliefs, while the optional user assessment is included in the final explanation solely as a comparison signal. This enables the system to highlight agreement or disagreement between user expectations and the computed assessment without compromising the integrity of the reasoning process.

\subsection{Hazard-Specific Symbolic Reasoning}

Hazard interpretation is performed by two specialised agents: the ForestAgent for wildfire cases and the WaterAgent for flood cases.

Despite operating on different EO indicators, both agents follow a common reasoning pattern. Starting from raw EO indicator beliefs, they derive intermediate evidence predicates, determine a support level, select a severity classification path, assign a confidence level, and produce an explicit conclusion status. This process is complemented by the generation of caveats, which qualify the interpretation when the available evidence is limited, ambiguous, or affected by temporal constraints.

In the current implementation, both agents also support a second-pass clarification stage. This stage can be triggered by the CoordinatorAgent when the initial assessment is uncertain or associated with significant caveats. During clarification, the agent provides additional symbolic information, such as primary limitations, strongest supporting evidence, and potential alternative interpretations, which are then incorporated into the final explanation.

\subsubsection{Wildfire Reasoning in \texttt{forest\_agent}}

The wildfire agent follows a layered reasoning strategy rather than relying on a single decision threshold. At the lowest level, raw EO indicators activate elementary evidence predicates that capture different aspects of fire impact, including vegetation damage, burn extent, and spectral burn response.\\

These low-level predicates are then combined into higher-level evidence patterns, such as coherent fire evidence, mixed evidence, or strong multisignal evidence. This intermediate abstraction allows the agent to distinguish between well-supported, partially supported, and ambiguous cases.

\begin{lstlisting}
vegetation_damage_signal(E) :-
    vegetation_loss_pct(E, VegetationLoss) &
    VegetationLoss >= 12.

burn_extent_signal(E) :-
    burned_area_pct(E, BurnedArea) &
    BurnedArea >= 3.

spectral_burn_signal(E) :-
    mean_dnbr(E, MeanDNBR) &
    MeanDNBR >= 0.04.

strong_multisignal_fire_evidence(E) :-
    strong_vegetation_damage(E) &
    strong_burn_extent(E) &
    strong_spectral_burn_signal(E).
\end{lstlisting}

Once an evidence pattern is established, the agent selects a corresponding severity path. Each rule encodes not only the severity level, but also a symbolic claim label, the list of supporting indicators, and the identifier of the rule that produced the conclusion.\\

\begin{lstlisting}
fire_severity_path(E, severe, strong_multisignal_fire_damage,
    [vegetation_loss_pct, burned_area_pct, mean_dnbr],
    rule_severe_fire_multisignal) :-
    strong_multisignal_fire_evidence(E).
\end{lstlisting}

This design makes the reasoning process fully explicit and traceable. In particular, it enables the system to differentiate between cases supported by multiple consistent signals, cases relying on a subset of indicators, and weaker cases driven by limited or partial evidence.\\

Following severity selection, the agent derives additional assessment dimensions. Confidence is computed from the overall strength and consistency of the supporting evidence, while caveats are introduced to explicitly represent limitations such as mixed signals or temporal uncertainty.\\

Importantly, inconclusive situations are handled directly within the reasoning layer. When the available evidence does not support a reliable hazard interpretation, the agent assigns an \texttt{undetermined} severity together with an explicit \texttt{inconclusive} conclusion status. This ensures that uncertainty is formally represented within the symbolic reasoning process, rather than being introduced only at the reporting stage.

\subsubsection{Support-Level Abstraction}

An intermediate symbolic abstraction is introduced between evidence synthesis and final decision. Rather than mapping evidence patterns directly to severity and confidence, the hazard agents first classify the available evidence in terms of a \emph{support level}.

The main symbolic categories are:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{strong}, for consistent multisignal evidence
\item \texttt{moderate}, for coherent but not fully reinforced evidence
\item \texttt{weak}, for limited or residual evidence
\item \texttt{conflicting}, for partially contradictory evidence
\item \texttt{insufficient}, for cases in which no reliable hazard-support pattern can be established
\end{itemize}

This abstraction layer separates \emph{evidence interpretation} from \emph{decision output}. As a result, severity and confidence are not derived through a direct mapping from numerical thresholds to labels.

Instead, the agents first construct a symbolic judgment describing how strongly the available evidence supports the hazard hypothesis. This intermediate representation is then used to derive severity, confidence, and conclusion status in a structured and explainable manner.

This design improves both modularity and interpretability. It allows different reasoning stages to remain conceptually distinct and ensures that final classifications can be traced back not only to individual indicators, but also to the overall strength and coherence of the supporting evidence.

\subsubsection{Flood Reasoning in \texttt{water\_agent}}

The flood agent follows a reasoning structure analogous to that of the wildfire agent. It starts from EO indicators such as \texttt{water\_increase\_pct/2}, \texttt{newly\_flooded\_area\_pct/2}, and \texttt{ndwi\_change/2}, and transforms them into symbolic flood evidence through a layered interpretation process.

At the lowest level, numerical indicators activate elementary signal predicates capturing different aspects of flooding, including water expansion, flood extent, and spectral water response. These signals are then combined into higher-level evidence patterns that distinguish between coherent, partial, residual, or conflicting flood signatures.

A key feature of the flood agent is its ability to represent residual and temporally constrained flood signals. This is particularly relevant for cases such as Ahr Valley, where the post-event acquisition occurs several days after the flood peak. In such situations, the EO signal may capture only the remaining inundation rather than the full extent of the event.

Rather than forcing a strong hazard classification, the agent explicitly models these cases as residual or weak evidence, often accompanied by caveats indicating possible underestimation. When the available signals are insufficient to support a reliable interpretation, the system produces an explicit inconclusive outcome.

As in the wildfire case, the output of the hazard agent is not a single label but a structured symbolic assessment capturing multiple dimensions of the reasoning process:

\begin{lstlisting}
hazard_assessment(E, Hazard, Severity, SupportLevel, ConclusionStatus,
    ConfidenceLevel, ClaimLabel, PrimaryCaveat, EvidenceList,
    CaveatList, RuleLabel)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection{Specialist Clarification Stage}

In addition to first-pass classification, hazard-specialized agents also support a second-stage clarification process. When the coordinator determines that a case remains uncertain, the same specialist agent is asked to refine its own assessment rather than delegating the task to a different agent.

This design preserves domain specialization while introducing a controlled cooperative feedback loop within the multi-agent system.

The clarification step is implemented as a secondary achievement goal:

\begin{lstlisting}
clarify_assessment(E, ClaimLabel, PrimaryCaveat, Requester)
\end{lstlisting}

Upon receiving this request, the specialist agent produces a structured symbolic clarification containing:

\begin{itemize}
\item the primary limitation affecting the initial interpretation
\item the strongest retained evidence supporting the claim
\item an optional alternative claim
\item the clarification status
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
clarification_result(E, ClaimLabel, PrimaryLimitation,
    StrongestEvidence, AlternativeClaim, ClarificationStatus)
\end{lstlisting}

As a result, the reasoning process is no longer strictly linear. In straightforward cases, the system proceeds directly from first-pass assessment to fusion. In more uncertain or caveat-heavy cases, the coordinator triggers a second reasoning step, allowing the specialist agent to explicitly qualify, refine, or reconsider its initial interpretation before the final integration stage.

\subsection{Coordinator Fusion}

The coordinator performs two complementary functions: orchestration and fusion.

First, the CoordinatorAgent scans all \texttt{event/1} beliefs, identifies the responsible specialist through \texttt{agent\_responsible/2}, and dispatches the goal \texttt{assess\_event(E, coordinator\_agent)} to the appropriate hazard agent.

Upon receiving a \texttt{hazard\_assessment(...)} message, the coordinator stores a structured set of derived beliefs capturing both the result and its provenance. In particular, it records the hazard outcome, a reasoning trace, and explicit support links:

\begin{lstlisting}
+hazard_result(E, Hazard, Severity, HazardConfidence,
    ClaimLabel, PrimaryCaveat, SourceAgent);
+hazard_trace(E, SourceAgent, RuleLabel, EvidenceList, CaveatList);
+claim_support(E, hazard_classification, SourceAgent, EvidenceList);
\end{lstlisting}

This representation ensures that each symbolic conclusion remains traceable to the evidence and rules that produced it.

Before proceeding to fusion, the coordinator evaluates whether the first-pass assessment is sufficiently reliable or whether a clarification step is required. This decision is based exclusively on internal symbolic signals—namely confidence levels and caveat labels—rather than on any external ground truth.

In the current implementation, clarification is triggered for low-confidence cases and for selected medium-confidence cases associated with specific caveats:

\begin{lstlisting}
clarification_required(low, _).
clarification_required(medium, burn_signal_weak).
clarification_required(medium, weak_water_signal).
clarification_required(medium, residual_observation_window).
\end{lstlisting}

If clarification is required, the coordinator stores a temporary \texttt{clarification\_context(...)} belief, sends a clarification request to the same specialist agent, and waits for a corresponding \texttt{clarification\_result(...)} message. Only after this second-pass refinement does the system proceed to fusion.

The fusion stage integrates hazard assessment with contextual exposure and caveat-aware confidence adjustment to derive a final symbolic interpretation. Four derived predicates play a central role in this process:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{concern\_level/3}, combining hazard severity and exposure
\item \texttt{fusion\_confidence/4}, refining confidence based on caveats
\item \texttt{interpretation\_mode/3}, qualifying the reliability of the interpretation
\item \texttt{case\_profile/4}, producing a compact integrated classification
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
population_exposure_class(E, ExposureClass);
concern_level(Severity, ExposureClass, ConcernLevel);
fusion_confidence(Severity, HazardConfidence, PrimaryCaveat, FusionConfidence);
interpretation_mode(FusionConfidence, PrimaryCaveat, InterpretationMode);
case_profile(ConcernLevel, FusionConfidence, InterpretationMode, Profile);
\end{lstlisting}

The final representation also records whether clarification occurred and, if so, which limitations and strongest evidence were identified during the second pass.

As a result, the system does not rely solely on EO-derived indicators. Instead, it produces an integrated interpretation that accounts for hazard severity, population exposure, evidence reliability, and potential uncertainty. For example, a severe flood affecting a high-exposure region is distinguished from a mild wildfire in a low-exposure area, even though both are derived from the same underlying reasoning framework.

Finally, the coordinator stores the conclusion status returned by the specialist and, when available, compares the inferred severity with the optional \texttt{user\_assessment/2} belief provided through the UI. This comparison does not influence the reasoning process itself, but is included in the final explanation to highlight agreement or disagreement between user expectations and system inference.

\subsection{Threshold Design and Limitations}

The thresholds used within the hazard agents should be interpreted as \emph{interpretable prototype cutoffs} rather than as operational EO standards. They were not introduced as arbitrary constants. Instead, they were selected as literature-informed heuristic ranges and subsequently tuned against the small case-study dataset used in this project, with the goal of enabling the symbolic layer to distinguish between strong, moderate, weak, conflicting, residual, and inconclusive evidence patterns in a consistent and interpretable manner.

More specifically, the thresholds are designed to:

\begin{itemize}
\item reflect commonly observed relative ranges for EO-derived water, vegetation, and burn indicators, without claiming universal validity
\item align symbolic rules with qualitative EO interpretation practices reported in the literature, where stronger hazard evidence typically requires consistent changes across multiple indicators
\item ensure separability within the case-study dataset, allowing the rule base to distinguish strong multisignal cases from weaker or partial evidence configurations
\item isolate residual, weak, or contradictory situations that should explicitly trigger caveats or clarification
\item preserve transparency and interpretability of the reasoning layer
\end{itemize}

While this design supports explainability, it also introduces clear limitations. The reasoning layer depends on manually defined thresholds, which may not generalize across different geographic regions, acquisition conditions, or hazard types. In addition, the system is sensitive to indicator noise and edge cases, particularly when EO signals are weak or temporally misaligned with the actual event peak.

Furthermore, the thresholds have not been calibrated on a large-scale benchmark dataset, and therefore cannot be considered statistically validated. They should instead be understood as scientifically motivated symbolic design choices, intended to support structured reasoning and explanation rather than to provide optimal predictive performance.

These limitations are acceptable within the scope of the project, which prioritizes interpretability, traceability, and controlled demonstration over operational deployment and large-scale generalization.

\subsection{Explanation Generation and Traceability}

The final stage of the reasoning layer is handled by the ExplanationAgent. This agent does not operate on raw EO indicators. Instead, it receives the fully integrated symbolic case produced by the coordinator, together with the associated rule label, evidence list, caveat structure, and optional clarification metadata.

In the current architecture, the explanation agent is not responsible for direct natural-language generation. Its role is to transform the integrated symbolic assessment into a structured semantic artifact that can be exported to the Python layer and reused by the ontology population and report generation components.

The resulting explanation payload preserves the full semantic structure of the reasoning process, including:

\begin{itemize}
\item event identity and geographic metadata
\item hazard classification, severity, conclusion status, concern level, confidence, and case profile
\item claim label, supporting evidence, and caveat structure
\item clarification status, strongest evidence, and alternative claim
\item provenance information, including the source agent and activated rule
\item optional user-assessment alignment metadata
\item an explicit \texttt{explanation\_trace(...)} capturing the reasoning path
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
semantic_explanation(E,
    event_frame(...),
    assessment_frame(...),
    evidence_frame(...),
    clarification_frame(...),
    provenance_frame(...),
    headline_frame(...),
    debug_text(...),
    explanation_trace(...))
\end{lstlisting}

This structured representation ensures that the final explanation is not a detached summary, but a fully traceable artifact grounded in the underlying symbolic reasoning process. Each component of the explanation can be linked back to the rule that was activated, the evidence predicates that supported the claim, the caveats that qualified the interpretation, and any clarification steps performed during reasoning.

The coordinator exports this payload as a JSON-compatible structure, allowing the downstream Python layer to consume and reuse the symbolic output without re-executing the reasoning process. In this way, the system cleanly separates reasoning, semantic structuring, and natural-language generation, while preserving end-to-end traceability.

\subsection{Conditional Cooperation}

The interaction model adopted in this architecture can be described as a \textbf{specialist multi-agent system with conditional cooperation}. Rather than implementing a fully deliberative or negotiation-heavy MAS where multiple agents contribute to every decision, the system relies on a staged interaction strategy.

In particular, two reasoning phases are defined:

\begin{enumerate}
\item a fast first-pass assessment performed by a single hazard-specialized agent
\item a second-pass clarification loop, activated only for uncertain or caveat-heavy cases
\end{enumerate}

This design ensures that the majority of events are processed efficiently through a direct specialist evaluation, while still allowing additional reasoning effort to be allocated selectively when the initial assessment is not sufficiently reliable.

From a system perspective, this approach balances efficiency and cooperation. The agents remain highly specialized and avoid unnecessary communication overhead, yet the architecture is not reduced to a purely linear pipeline. Instead, it supports a targeted form of interaction in which agents can refine their own conclusions when prompted by the coordinator.

As a result, the system achieves a form of \emph{conditional cooperation}, where interaction is driven by epistemic uncertainty rather than enforced by design. This makes the reasoning process both scalable and explainable, while preserving the clarity of specialist-driven interpretations.

\subsection{Role of the Reasoning Layer}

The reasoning layer is the core component that transforms EO-derived indicators into structured and explainable environmental interpretations. Its role extends beyond simple hazard classification. Instead, it provides a fully transparent symbolic representation of how conclusions are derived from EO evidence.

More specifically, the reasoning layer makes explicit:

\begin{itemize}
\item the belief structure associated with each event, including metadata, EO indicators, and contextual information
\item the threshold-based conditions under which numerical indicators activate symbolic evidence predicates
\item the selection of severity paths by hazard-specialized agents, together with the corresponding claim labels and supporting evidence
\item the generation of caveats that qualify or reduce confidence based on signal quality and temporal uncertainty
\item the conditions under which the coordinator triggers a second-pass clarification loop
\item the fusion process combining hazard assessment with population exposure and caveat-aware confidence
\item the inclusion of clarification outcomes within the final interpretation
\item the construction of a fully traceable explanation linked to the underlying symbolic reasoning process
\end{itemize}

As a result, the reasoning layer does not operate as a black-box classifier. Instead, it acts as an explicit semantic bridge between EO measurements and human-interpretable explanations, ensuring that every conclusion can be traced back to a well-defined set of beliefs, rules, and interaction steps.

\section{NLP Layer}

After the Jason reasoning layer produces a \texttt{semantic\_explanation(...)} structure, the system enters a Python-based post-processing pipeline. The role of this layer is to transform the symbolic output into a reusable semantic representation, populate the ontology, validate and query the resulting semantic graph, and generate readable reports.

The NLP layer is therefore best understood as a \textbf{semantic transformation and explanation delivery layer}, rather than as a second reasoning engine. It does not access raw beliefs from the specialist agents. Instead, it operates exclusively on the structured explanation artifact produced by the \texttt{explanation\_agent}, which acts as the bridge between symbolic reasoning and downstream components.

The pipeline is implemented as a modular sequence of Python scripts. Conceptually, the flow progresses from raw Jason JSON to a transformed semantic JSON representation, which is then used to populate the ontology. The resulting semantic graph is subsequently validated and queried, and finally converted into an English report.

Within this architecture, the NLP layer performs four tightly connected functions: it normalizes the Jason output into a cleaner semantic structure, instantiates the EO2Explain ontology, validates and queries the populated semantic graph, and generates readable event reports. These steps are sequential in execution but conceptually unified as a single transformation from symbolic reasoning output to human-interpretable explanation.

\subsection{Transformation of Jason Output}

The first stage of the pipeline processes the raw JSON exported from Jason, corresponding to the \texttt{semantic\_explanation(...)} payload produced by the ExplanationAgent.

Although this representation faithfully preserves the symbolic structure, it remains closely tied to Jason’s internal term format, using nested \texttt{functor}, \texttt{predicate}, and \texttt{terms} fields. This makes it difficult to use directly in downstream components.

To address this, the transformer performs a \textbf{semantic normalization step}. It decodes Jason terms, unwraps nested frames, and converts the representation into a flatter and more accessible JSON structure. The result is a schema organised into explicit sections such as event metadata, assessment, evidence, clarification, provenance, and trace information.

This transformed representation serves as the main interface between the MAS and the NLP components. It preserves the full semantics of the original explanation while enabling simple key-based access to relevant fields, without requiring traversal of nested symbolic structures.

Importantly, the transformation preserves the MAS-derived \texttt{conclusion\_status}, ensuring that explicitly inconclusive outcomes remain distinguishable from confirmed hazard classifications throughout the pipeline.

\subsection{Ontology Population}

The second stage of the pipeline is ontology population. The system uses \texttt{Owlready2} to load the EO2Explain ontology and instantiate it dynamically from the transformed semantic JSON.

This design turns the ontology into an \textbf{active semantic layer} rather than a static artifact. For each event, the population script creates or updates a set of event-scoped individuals, including the event itself, its assessment, the associated evidence bundle, the clarification object, the provenance record, and the explanation trace. In parallel, shared individuals are created or reused to represent controlled vocabulary concepts such as hazards, severity levels, confidence levels, exposure levels, concern levels, evidence items, caveats, claim labels, rules, and agents.

As a result, the ontology captures both event-specific structures and reusable semantic concepts within a unified representation. Assessment-level attributes such as \texttt{support\_level} and \texttt{conclusion\_status} are preserved explicitly, ensuring that the outcome of the symbolic reasoning process is fully retained.

From a structural perspective, the ontology encodes not only the final decision but also how that decision was constructed. Each event is linked to an assessment, which in turn is connected to its evidence bundle, clarification, and provenance. The explanation trace provides a compact but explicit summary of the reasoning path, preserving the link between symbolic inference and semantic representation.

After population, the ontology supports both semantic validation and structured querying. Validation ensures that the populated graph satisfies key consistency constraints, such as the presence of exactly one assessment per event and single-valued core properties, while also preventing logically inconsistent combinations, for example assigning high confidence to explicitly inconclusive cases. Querying enables the extraction of aggregate views over the dataset, such as identifying severe flood events, low-confidence cases, clarification-triggered assessments, or situations characterized by weak or conflicting support.

However, the ontology is not used to generate the final report text. Report generation operates directly on the transformed semantic JSON. The role of the ontology is instead to provide a validated and queryable semantic layer that supports inspection, consistency checking, and future extensions, including semantic interoperability and knowledge sharing.
\subsection{Report Generation}

The final stage of the pipeline is report generation. A dedicated Python module converts the transformed semantic JSON into readable English text.

This component is implemented as a \textbf{deterministic natural language generation (NLG) pipeline}. Rather than directly exposing symbolic labels, it performs a structured transformation from a formal semantic representation into a human-readable explanation. The generation process follows a sequence of stages in which relevant semantic elements are first selected, then organized into a fixed rhetorical structure, subsequently mapped into domain-specific phrases, and finally assembled into coherent text.

The resulting report is organized into sections such as summary, event description, assessment, supporting evidence, caveats, clarification, and provenance. Within this structure, the system communicates what happened, how severe the event is, whether the outcome corresponds to a confirmed hazard classification or an explicitly inconclusive case, and how confident the system is in its interpretation. It also explains which evidence supports the assessment, which caveats limit its reliability, whether a second-pass clarification was required, and whether the inferred result aligns with any user-provided expectation. The provenance of the decision is explicitly reported, including the responsible agent and the rule that produced the conclusion.

Importantly, this layer does not generate free text from scratch. Instead, it verbalizes a fully specified semantic structure produced by the MAS and normalized by the transformation stage. The report is therefore not an independent interpretation, but a faithful linguistic rendering of an already explicit symbolic explanation. As a result, the final output remains fully traceable, since each statement in the report can be linked back to specific symbolic elements such as evidence predicates, caveats, clarification results, and rule-level provenance.

\subsection{Implementation Structure}

From an implementation perspective, the NLP layer is organized into four lightweight and clearly separated modules:

\begin{itemize}
\item a transformer that converts raw Jason JSON into normalized semantic JSON
\item a loader that reads transformed payloads for downstream components
\item an ontology population module based on \texttt{Owlready2}
\item a report generator that verbalizes the semantic payloads as English reports
\end{itemize}

These modules are orchestrated by a single pipeline script, allowing the entire post-MAS workflow to be executed end-to-end in a single step. This modular design simplifies maintenance while making the separation of concerns explicit.

At the architectural level, the Jason MAS remains responsible for reasoning, while the Python layer handles semantic transformation, ontology population, validation, and explanation delivery. The overall process can therefore be understood as a structured progression:

\begin{center}
symbolic case $\rightarrow$ semantic explanation payload $\rightarrow$ transformed semantic JSON $\rightarrow$ ontology population $\rightarrow$ report generation
\end{center}

This separation improves extensibility. The same semantic payload can be reused for ontology-based querying, future NLP enhancements, or alternative report-generation strategies without requiring any modification to the hazard reasoning logic implemented in Jason.

It also strengthens the explainability of the system. The MAS is responsible for determining the meaning of the case, while the NLP layer is responsible for how that meaning is represented, formalized, validated, and communicated. This clear division ensures that reasoning and explanation remain conceptually distinct yet tightly integrated within the overall architecture.

\subsection{Execution and Reproducibility}

The prototype can be executed through a single wrapper script that orchestrates the EO, MAS, ontology, and NLP stages. In the default configuration, precomputed EO indicators are used, allowing the symbolic reasoning and explanation pipeline to be reproduced without recomputing satellite-derived features.

The system requires a Python environment (3.10 or later), a Java JDK for the Jason MAS, and a small set of Python dependencies (including \texttt{flask} and \texttt{owlready2}). The full pipeline can be launched with a single command:

\begin{lstlisting}
python3 scripts/run_project.py
\end{lstlisting}

Further setup details, including full dependency lists and optional EO recomputation, are provided in the project repository.

\section{Conclusion}

This project demonstrates how symbolic AI can provide an explicit interpretability layer over Earth Observation (EO) evidence. Rather than mapping pixel-level indicators directly to opaque classifications, the system makes the reasoning process transparent by exposing detected signals, applied rule patterns, caveats affecting confidence, and clarification steps. In this way, EO2Explain shows how symbolic multi-agent reasoning can support explainable hazard assessment.

From an architectural perspective, the system is modular and extensible. The separation between coordination, hazard-specific reasoning, and explanation generation enables the integration of additional hazard-specialized agents without altering the overall interaction model. The shared reasoning structure adopted by the WaterAgent and ForestAgent illustrates how new domains can be incorporated by adapting rules and indicators while preserving the same communication pattern.

However, the current implementation also presents several limitations. The reliance on a central CoordinatorAgent introduces a potential bottleneck as the system scales. Furthermore, the rule-based nature of the agents, while essential for interpretability, limits flexibility and requires manual design and maintenance when extending to new hazard types. Compared to sub-symbolic approaches, the system is therefore less suited for predictive generalization and large-scale data-driven adaptation.

In addition, the ontology layer is not yet fully exploited. While it provides a structured semantic representation and supports validation and querying, its role remains largely supportive. Its full potential would emerge in larger deployments involving multiple coordinated agents and richer knowledge integration.

Future work could address these limitations by exploring distributed coordination mechanisms, hybrid symbolic–subsymbolic approaches, and more scalable knowledge representations. Further extensions may include larger and more diverse datasets, improved calibration of reasoning thresholds, and more advanced ontology-driven querying and explanation strategies.

Overall, EO2Explain shows that combining symbolic multi-agent systems with structured natural language generation can produce EO interpretations that are not only informative, but also transparent, traceable, and semantically grounded.
